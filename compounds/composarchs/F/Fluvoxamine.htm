Fluvoxamine

Fluvoxamine, sold under the brand name Luvox among others, is an antidepressant of the selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor (SSRI) class[5] which is used primarily for the treatment of obsessiveâ€“compulsive disorder (OCD).[6] It is also used to treat depression and anxiety disorders, such as panic disorder, social anxiety disorder, and post-traumatic stress disorder.[7][8]

Not to be confused with fluoxetine.

    Medical uses

    Fluvoxamine is approved in the United States for OCD,[9][6] and social anxiety disorder.[10] In other countries (e.g. Australia,[11][12] the UK,[13] and Russia[14]) it also has indications for major depressive disorder. In Japan it is currently approved to treat OCD, SAD and MDD.[15][16] Fluvoxamine is indicated for children and adolescents with OCD.[17] The drug works long-term, and retains its therapeutic efficacy for at least one year.[18] It has also been found to possess some analgesic properties in line with other SSRIs and tricyclic antidepressants.[19][20][21]

    There is tentative evidence that fluvoxamine is effective for social phobia in adults.[22] Fluvoxamine is also effective for GAD, SAD, panic disorder and separation anxiety disorder in children and adolescents.[23] There is tentative evidence that fluvoxamine may help some people with negative symptoms of chronic schizophrenia.[24][25]

    Interactions
    Luvox (fluvoxamine) 100 mg film-coated scored tablets

    Fluvoxamine inhibits the following cytochrome P450 enzymes:[30][31][32][33][34][35][36][37][38]

    • CYP1A2 (strongly) which metabolizes agomelatine, amitriptyline, caffeine, clomipramine, clozapine, duloxetine, haloperidol, imipramine, phenacetin, tacrine, tamoxifen, theophylline, olanzapine, etc.
    • CYP3A4 (moderately) which metabolizes alprazolam, aripiprazole, clozapine, haloperidol, quetiapine, pimozide, ziprasidone, etc.[39]
    • CYP2D6 (moderately) which metabolizes aripiprazole, chlorpromazine, clozapine, codeine, fluoxetine, haloperidol, olanzapine, oxycodone, paroxetine, perphenazine, pethidine, risperidone, sertraline, thioridazine, zuclopenthixol, etc.[39]
    • CYP2C9 (moderately) which metabolizes nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, phenytoin, sulfonylureas, etc.
    • CYP2C19 (strongly) which metabolizes clonazepam, diazepam, phenytoin, etc.
    • CYP2B6 (weakly) which metabolizes bupropion, cyclophosphamide, sertraline, tamoxifen, valproate, etc.

    By so doing, fluvoxamine can increase serum concentration of the substrates of these enzymes.[30]

    The plasma levels of oxidatively metabolized benzodiazepines (e.g., triazolam, midazolam, alprazolam and diazepam) are likely to be increased when co-administered with fluvoxamine. However the clearance of benzodiazepines metabolized by glucuronidation (e.g., lorazepam, oxazepam, temazepam)[40][41] is unlikely to be affected by fluvoxamine.[42] It appears that benzodiazepines metabolized by nitro-reduction (clonazepam, nitrazepam) are unlikely to be affected by fluvoxamine.[43] Using fluvoxamine and alprazolam together can increase alprazolam plasma concentrations.[44] If alprazolam is coadministered with fluvoxamine, the initial alprazolam dose should be reduced to the lowest effective dose.[45][46]

    Fluvoxamine and ramelteon coadministration is not indicated.[47][48]

    Fluvoxamine has been observed to increase serum concentrations of mirtazapine, which is mainly metabolized by CYP1A2, CYP2D6, and CYP3A4, by 3- to 4-fold in humans.[49] Caution and adjustment of dosage as necessary are warranted when combining fluvoxamine and mirtazapine.[49]

    Fluvoxamine seriously affects the pharmacokinetics of tizanidine and increases the intensity and duration of its effects. Because of the potentially hazardous consequences, the concomitant use of tizanidine with fluvoxamine, or other potent inhibitors of CYP1A2, should be avoided.[50]

    Fluvoxamine interaction with St John's wort can lead to increased serotonin levels and potentially lead to Serotonin syndrome.

    Fluvoxamine is a potent selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor with around 100-fold affinity for the serotonin transporter over the norepinephrine transporter.[31] It has negligible affinity for the dopamine transporter or any other site, with the sole exception of the Ïƒ1 receptor.[54][55] It behaves as a potent agonist at this receptor and has the highest affinity (36 nM) of any SSRI for doing so.[54] This may contribute to its antidepressant and anxiolytic effects and may also afford it some efficacy in treating the cognitive symptoms of depression.[56] Contrary to Fluoxetine, Fluvoxamine metabolites are inactive, without a significant effect on serotonin or norepinephrine uptake.[57] In 2019 fluvoxamine was shown to modulate the sigma-1 receptorâ€“IRE1 pathway in mice and provide an unexpected benefit in preclinical models of inflammation and sepsis.

    History

    Fluvoxamine was developed by Kali-Duphar,[58] part of Solvay Pharmaceuticals, Belgium, now Abbott Laboratories, and introduced as Floxyfral in Switzerland in 1983.[58] It was approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in 1994, and introduced as Luvox in the US.[59] In India, it is available, among several other brands, as Uvox by Abbott.[60] It was one of the first SSRI antidepressants to be launched, and is prescribed in many countries to patients with major depression.[61] It was the first SSRI, a non-TCA drug, approved by the U.S. FDA specifically for the treatment of OCD.[62] At the end of 1995, more than ten million patients worldwide had been treated with fluvoxamine.[63] Fluvoxamine was the first SSRI to be registered for the treatment of obsessive compulsive disorder in children by the FDA in 1997.[64] In Japan, fluvoxamine was the first SSRI to be approved for the treatment of depression in 1999[65][66] and was later in 2005 the first drug to be approved for the treatment of social anxiety disorder.[67] Fluvoxamine was the first SSRI approved for clinical use in the United Kingdom.[68] During the COVID-19 pandemic it underwent several trials of drug repurposing research against preventing cytokine storms, a complication that had arisen in severe cases of COVID-19.[69]

    Society and culture

    Manufacturers include BayPharma, Synthon, and Teva, among others.[70] The largest manufacturer is Synthon BV.[71]
